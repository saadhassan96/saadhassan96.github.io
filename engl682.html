Designing accessible and inclusive natural language processing (NLP) systems requires understanding nuanced conceptualizations of social attitudes that may be represented in the underlying machine-learning (ML) systems. These representations in models can be thecause of inadvertent and undesirable perpetuation of social biases. Ableism is one such bias which is rarely addressed in ML models andcommunities of people with disabilities are often neglected when designing ML models for NLP systems [2, 8, 5, 7, 4, 3, 1]. The bias inthese systems can stem from both the dataset and its annotation as well as from ML model specifications. To better understand the biases related to disabilities in NLP systems, we extend the work of [6] by quantitatively and qualitatively accessing the bias in NLP models that stems fromdatasets used,text representations employed, andmodel specifications.The contributionof this work are (1) examining where ableist biases in NLP models stem from and (2) proposing and experimentally evaluating methods to remove those biases. We investigate these promising avenues of research in the following research questions:

1. Can the choice of corpus used in designing an NLP model explain an observed abelist bias in the model?(RQ1)

2. Do (a) text representations and (b) language models that feed NLP systems for downstream applications perpetuate ableist biases?(RQ2)

3. Does a proposed debiasing solution help significantly remove ableist bias in an NLP system?(RQ3)

To answer RQ1, we will take a large dataset, e.g., Jigsaw unintended Bias dataset (Jigsaw,2019), and extract equal samples of toxicand nontoxic comments in data that mentions disability and data that does not. We will find unigrams and non-adjacent bigrams thatare statistically more likely to appear in comments that mention disability using log-likelihoods. We will then manually inspect andcategorize them. To answer RQ2a, we will train BERT for word prediction using prepared statements. The prepared statements would be of three types: mentioning disabilities, mentioning disabilities along with other social biases, and not mention disabilities. Afterward,we will do sentiment analysis on all the resultant statements. This will be complemented by a qualitative analysis of generated sentences as well. For answering RQ2b, we will identify recommended and non-recommended linguistic expressions used to refer to people withdisabilities. We will generate two datasets that incorporate these expressions and perform text classification tasks on the resultant datae.g. topic modeling and intent detection. We will compare the performance at text classification for both datasets. Finally, we will propose and experimentally evaluate the performance of a debiasing method on a downstream task. We will primarily use the same dataset ([6]) that was used in [3]. It consists of online comments that mention individuals with physical,intellectual, learning, psychiatric disability or mental illness.
